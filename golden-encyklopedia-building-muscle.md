# The Golden Encyclopedia: Building Muscle (The Complete Guide)

## TL;DR — The 8 Core Rules

1. **Volume is king** — total weekly sets matter most for muscle growth
2. **Train each muscle 2x per week** — beats 1x per week
3. **Load barely matters** — light and heavy weights build muscle equally (if you train to failure)
4. **Heavy weights win for strength** — 1RM strength needs heavy loads
5. **Protein: eat 1.62 g per kg of bodyweight minimum** — this is the ceiling for muscle gain
6. **During fat loss, eat 2.4 g per kg** — preserves muscle while cutting calories
7. **Cardio + lifting can interfere** — same-session cardio may blunt muscle gains
8. **You can build muscle while losing fat** — but protein intake matters critically

## How Muscle Actually Grows

### The Simple Version

When you lift weights, muscle fibers tear microscopically. Your body repairs them and adds extra protein to prevent the same damage next time. Repeat this stimulus often enough, and fibers get bigger.

Three things trigger this response:
1. **Volume** — how many sets you do per week (THE primary driver)
2. **Frequency** — how many times per week you work the muscle (2x beats 1x)
3. **Tension** — heavy enough weight to be challenging (but light works too if you go to failure)

The molecular machinery (mTOR, protein synthesis) responds to this repeating damage. More volume, more frequency = more growth stimulus.

## Rule #1: Volume Is Everything

### The Science

Muscle hypertrophy follows a **clear dose-response curve to training volume**. More sets per week = more growth, up to a point.

**Practical numbers for hypertrophy:**
- **Minimum effective dose**: ~6–10 sets per muscle per week
- **Optimal range**: **12–20 sets per muscle per week**
- **Diminishing returns**: Beyond ~20 sets per week, gains plateau

### What This Means For You

If you weigh 79 kg:
- For **biceps**: aim for **12–16 total sets per week**
- For **chest**: aim for **15–20 total sets per week**
- For **back**: aim for **15–20 total sets per week**
- For **quads**: aim for **12–18 total sets per week**

You can do this in ONE session (e.g., 12 sets in one gym day) or SPLIT across two (e.g., 6 sets Monday + 6 sets Thursday).

**Volume tracking**: Count all sets (including warmups if they're close to working weight). Rest-pause sets, dropsets, and supersets count as separate sets.

## Rule #2: Train Each Muscle 2x Per Week

### The Science

Training a muscle **2x per week is superior to 1x per week** for hypertrophy, when total volume is equated. This is one of the most consistent findings in resistance training research.

Why? More frequent stimulus = more total growth stimulus window during the week.

### What This Means For You

Don't do all your chest work in one session. Split it:
- **Option A**: Upper/Lower split
  - Day 1: Chest, back, shoulders, arms (70 sets)
  - Day 2: Quads, hamstrings, glutes, calves (60 sets)
  - Day 3: Rest
  - Day 4: Upper/Lower repeated

- **Option B**: Push/Pull/Legs
  - Day 1: Chest, shoulders, triceps (push) — 2x/week
  - Day 2: Back, biceps (pull) — 2x/week
  - Day 3: Quads, hamstrings, glutes (legs) — 2x/week

Both work. The point: **each muscle should see a training stimulus at least twice per week**.

## Rule #3: Load (Light vs. Heavy) Doesn't Matter Much For Hypertrophy

### The Science

**If you train to failure, hypertrophy is roughly equal across light and heavy loads.**

Study findings:
- Light weight (12–20 reps) to failure: builds muscle
- Heavy weight (3–6 reps) to failure: builds muscle equally
- The difference is negligible when reps to failure are matched

Heavy loads do have one advantage: **easier to reach high volume without fatigue**. You can do 20 sets of squats at 85% 1RM more comfortably than 20 sets at 50% 1RM.

### What This Means For You

You don't need to squat heavy to build leg muscle. You can:
- Squat 315 lbs for 5 reps (3 sets = 15 total reps)
- OR squat 185 lbs for 12 reps (3 sets = 36 total reps to failure)

Both build muscle. The heavy version is less taxing on your CNS and joints. The light version is less equipment-dependent.

**Practical rule**: Use a weight you can control for 6–12 reps per set, reach near-failure on most sets, and accumulate volume.

## Rule #4: Heavy Weights ARE Better For 1RM Strength

This is critical: **hypertrophy doesn't require heavy loads, but 1RM strength does.**

If your goal is to add 50 lbs to your squat max:
- Light loads won't get you there
- Heavy loads (80%+ 1RM) build maximal strength

**For your goals (calisthenics skills: muscle ups, handstands):**
- Some heavy strength work helps
- But hypertrophy (muscle size) from moderate loads also helps
- Skill practice (handstand holds, muscle-up negatives) matters most

## Rule #5: Protein — The Threshold Is 1.62 g per kg Bodyweight

### The Science

Protein supplementation aids muscle and strength gains. The effect plateaus at approximately **1.62 g/kg/day total protein** intake (from all food sources, not just supplements).

Beyond ~1.62 g/kg/day, additional protein provides **no extra benefit for muscle building**.

### For Your 79 kg Bodyweight

- **Minimum**: 1.62 g/kg × 79 kg = **128 g/day**
- **Your current intake**: 170 g/day = 2.15 g/kg (above threshold, which is fine)

You don't *need* 170 g/day for muscle gain alone. But there's a nuance:

## Rule #6: During Fat Loss, Eat 2.4 g per kg

### The Science

When eating in a caloric deficit (cutting fat), protein requirements increase. A study on recomposition (simultaneous fat loss + muscle gain) found:

**2.4 g protein/kg/day + resistance training + moderate caloric deficit = muscle gain + fat loss**

Why the increase? Deficit-induced muscle breakdown is suppressed more effectively at higher protein intake. You need the extra protein to shield muscle during the cut.

### For Your 79 kg Bodyweight

- **For cutting (your current phase)**: 2.4 g/kg × 79 kg = **190 g/day**
- **Your current intake**: 170 g/day (slightly below optimal for your goal)

**Recommendation**: Raise protein to ~190 g/day on your gym and running days (currently eating 2,400–2,600 calories). On rest days, 170 g is acceptable since activity is lower.

## Rule #7: Protein Timing Probably Doesn't Matter (But We Don't Have Evidence Either Way)

The papers in this collection don't address meal timing or pre/post-workout nutrition specifically. The data shows that **total daily protein intake matters**; the evidence for timing is weak in the literature reviewed here.

Conservative approach: distribute protein evenly across meals (40–50 g per meal × 4–5 meals).

## Rule #8: Can You Build Muscle While Losing Fat?

### The Science

Yes. A caloric deficit + high protein + resistance training + adequate recovery = **recomposition** (muscle gain + fat loss simultaneously).

This is most effective if:
1. You're new to training (you've trained ~10 years, so you're not a beginner, but recomposition still works)
2. You have adequate protein (2.4 g/kg — see Rule #6)
3. You maintain training volume and intensity
4. You're in a *moderate* deficit (not extreme)

### For You Right Now

You're 3 kg down in 2 months (0.375 kg/week). This is a healthy rate. At **170 g protein + 4 gym days/week + resistance training**, you're likely preserving most muscle while losing fat.

**To optimize**: raise protein to 190 g on gym/run days and maintain your current training volume.

## Rule #9: Cardio + Lifting in the Same Session Can Interfere

### The Science

Doing cardio and resistance training in the **same session** can blunt muscle and strength gains via molecular interference (mTOR signaling, protein synthesis pathways).

Separate sessions (same day or different days) eliminate this interference.

### What This Means For You

Your current schedule is good:
- **Days 1–4**: Lifting only (no cardio in these sessions)
- **Day 5**: Running (separate day)

If you wanted to add conditioning, do it:
- On a separate day from lifting
- OR at least 6+ hours apart from lifting
- OR on a low-volume lifting day

**Your schedule avoids the interference problem. Keep it as is.**

## Rule #10: Strength vs. Hypertrophy — Which Should You Focus On?

### The Science

- **Strength** (1RM maxes) responds best to heavy loads (80%+ 1RM) and lower reps (1–5)
- **Hypertrophy** (muscle size) responds to volume (total sets) across a wide load range (light to heavy)

They're not mutually exclusive. Hypertrophy supports strength; strength supports hypertrophy through increased load capacity.

### For Your Goals

You want calisthenics skills (muscle ups, handstands, etc.):
- These require **relative strength** (strength relative to bodyweight)
- Building muscle mass (hypertrophy) helps
- Skill-specific practice (progressions, holds, negatives) matters most

**Recommendation**: Maintain hypertrophy work (12–20 sets/muscle/week) + add strength work (5–8 heavy sets/week on main lifts) + dedicate specific time to skill practice.

## FAQ

**Q: Should I bulk or cut first?**
A: You're cutting now (good). Once you reach your target body fat or stop losing weight, transition to a maintenance phase and lean bulk (surplus: +200–300 cal/day) to build size for calisthenics skills.

**Q: Do I need to track macros perfectly?**
A: Track protein (the main variable). Carbs and fats matter for energy and hormones, but protein is the critical one for the recomposition phase you're in.

**Q: What if I can't hit 190 g protein?**
A: 170 g is acceptable (still above the 128 g minimum). 190 g is *optimized for your cutting goal*. More is rarely harmful.

**Q: Can I skip rest days?**
A: No. Recovery is where adaptation happens. Your 2 rest days per week are important for muscle protein synthesis and CNS recovery.

**Q: Should I lift heavy or light?**
A: Both work for hypertrophy. Choose based on joint health and preference. Given your shoulder history (overextension), moderate loads may be safer than very heavy ones.

**Q: Does protein type matter (whey vs. casein vs. whole food)?**
A: No. Total protein intake is what matters. Whey and whole food are equally effective.

**Q: How often should I change my routine?**
A: When you stop progressing (can't add reps or sets for 2–3 weeks). Training age ~10 years means you can use the same routine for 4–12 weeks.
