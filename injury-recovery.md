# Injury Prevention & Recovery Guide

## TL;DR — The 6 Recovery Rules

1. **Isometric holds reduce tendon pain** — 5 sets × 45 seconds at 70% effort kills pain for 45+ minutes
2. **Train injured tissue at long muscle lengths** — produces most hypertrophy + tendon adaptation
3. **Massage is the #1 recovery modality** — best for soreness and fatigue (evidence-backed)
4. **Cold exposure (ice baths) reduces inflammation** — but may blunt muscle gains; use selectively
5. **Compression garments help with perceived fatigue** — minor effect, low cost, easy win
6. **Cutting calories slows recovery** — eat enough protein (2.0–2.4 g/kg/day) and get sleep

---

## Part 1: Understanding Tendon and Joint Injuries

### What Happens When a Tendon Hurts

Tendons are the rope-like tissue connecting muscle to bone. They get inflamed, irritated, or develop micro-tears from:

- **Overload**: too much volume, too fast
- **Underload**: tendon weakens from disuse, then a heavy load shocks it
- **Bad form**: shifting load to non-ideal angles
- **Joint extension past safe range**: shoulder overextension (your case)

Pain is a warning, but it's not always proportional to damage. Sometimes pain lingers after tissue has healed (sensitization).

### Two Common Injury Types

**Acute** — sudden onset (e.g., shoulder overextension from a missed dip or a slip at home)
- Initial 48 hrs: rest, ice, anti-inflammatories if needed
- Days 3–14: gentle movement, avoid the offending position
- Weeks 2–8: progressive loading (this is where most people fail)

**Chronic / overuse** — gradual onset (e.g., bicep tendonitis from overhead pressing)
- Rarely "rests itself away"
- Requires loading the tissue progressively
- Pain-free angles + isometric holds are gold

## Part 2: The Isometric Rehab Protocol (Evidence-Based)

### What Is an Isometric Hold?

A muscle contraction with **no joint movement**. You hold a position under load. No reps, no range — just a static contraction.

Examples: wall sit (quad), plank (core), cable external rotation hold (rotator cuff).

### The Pain-Reducing Protocol

A single bout of isometric exercise can reduce tendon pain by ~87% for 45+ minutes via central nervous system mechanisms.

| Variable | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Sets | **5** |
| Hold duration | **45 seconds per set** |
| Intensity | **70% of max voluntary contraction** (MVC) |
| Rest between sets | **2 minutes** |
| Frequency | **Daily** during pain phase |
| Position | **Pain-free joint angle** |

### How to Find 70% MVC

You don't need a strength meter:

- 0% = no effort
- 50% = "I can hold this all day"
- **70% = "I can hold this for 45–60 seconds"**
- 85% = "shaking after 20 seconds"
- 100% = max

70% MVC = a hard but sustainable hold.

### Why It Works

1. **Central pain modulation** — isometric contraction increases descending inhibition in the spinal cord, reducing pain signals
2. **Tendon loading** — gradual stress strengthens collagen organization

### Long-Length Bias

Holding at **longer muscle lengths** (e.g., shoulder fully stretched in external rotation) produces **more hypertrophy and tendon remodeling** than short-length holds. If pain allows, train near end-range.

## Part 3: Shoulder Rehab — Specific Drills

### Background: Shoulder Overextension

Most common pattern: heavy load + arm extended past safe range → stress on the anterior capsule, biceps tendon, or rotator cuff. Healing path: load progressively in **safe ranges**, gradually increasing range as pain decreases.

### Drill 1: Cable External Rotation Hold

- Cable at elbow height, elbow tucked, arm bent 90°
- Pull cable outward (rotating shoulder externally)
- Hold at end-range for 45 sec
- 5 sets × 45 sec, 2-min rest
- Start 5–10 lbs, progress to 70% MVC

**Why**: Strengthens infraspinatus and teres minor — the rotator cuff muscles that protect against overextension.

### Drill 2: Banded Shoulder Hold (Mid-Range)

- Band anchored at chest height
- Stand perpendicular to anchor, band in opposite hand
- Pull arm out to side, hold position 45 sec
- 5 sets × 45 sec, 2-min rest

**Why**: Trains stability in mid-range — the position you're in during heavy bench, rows, and overhead pressing.

### Drill 3: Wall Slide (Mobility + Stability)

- Stand with back to wall, arms in goalpost (90° at shoulders and elbows)
- Slowly slide arms up the wall, keep back/arms in contact
- Hold top 30 sec, slide down
- 3 sets

**Why**: Restores scapular upward rotation and shoulder mobility without high load.

### Drill 4: Prone Y / T / W Holds

- Lie face down on bench or floor
- **Y position**: arms in Y, thumbs up, lift, hold 20 sec
- **T position**: arms straight to sides, lift, hold 20 sec
- **W position**: elbows bent 90°, lift, squeeze blades, hold 20 sec
- 2 rounds

**Why**: Activates lower trap, mid trap, rhomboids — postural muscles that support the shoulder joint.

### When to Do These

- **During acute pain**: daily, before any pressing
- **Maintenance**: 2–3× per week, before upper body sessions
- **Before pressing**: 1 set as activation prep

## Part 4: Movements to Avoid (During Acute Shoulder Pain)

### Temporarily Banned (Until Pain-Free)

| Exercise | Why |
|----------|-----|
| Dips | Deep shoulder extension, mimics overextension |
| Straight-bar dips | Same + wrist/shoulder stress |
| Behind-the-neck press | Extreme external rotation under load |
| Behind-the-neck pulldown | Same risk |
| Heavy chest flyes (deep stretch) | Re-aggravates anterior capsule |
| Upright rows | Compresses rotator cuff |
| Heavy bench with elbows flared | Stresses anterior shoulder |

### Safe Replacements

| Avoid | Replace With |
|-------|--------------|
| Dips | Close-grip bench, machine triceps press |
| Straight-bar dips | Floor press, cable triceps extensions |
| Behind-neck press | Front overhead press, landmine press |
| Behind-neck pulldown | Front lat pulldown (neutral or pronated) |
| Deep chest flyes | Cable crossovers (limit range), pec deck (limited stretch) |
| Upright rows | Lateral raises, face pulls |
| Flared-elbow bench | Tucked-elbow bench (45° elbow), DB bench |

### Re-Introduction Criteria

A movement can return when:
1. Pain-free during the rehab drills
2. No pain during pressing/pulling for 2 weeks
3. Full range of motion restored
4. Light variation tolerated for 2 sessions before adding load

## Part 5: Post-Exercise Recovery (Evidence-Based Ranking)

| Modality | DOMS | Fatigue | Inflammation | Time |
|----------|------|---------|--------------|------|
| **Massage** | **★★★★★** | **★★★★★** | ★★★ | 20–60 min |
| Cold water immersion | ★★★ | ★★★ | ★★★★ | 10–15 min |
| Cold + Massage | ★★★★ | ★★★★ | ★★★★★ | 30–45 min |
| Compression garments | ★★ | ★★★ | ★★ | passive |
| Stretching | ★★ | ★ | ★ | 10–20 min |
| Active recovery | ★★ | ★★ | ★★ | 20–40 min |
| Electrostimulation | ★★ | ★★ | ★★ | 15–30 min |

### What This Means

**Massage is the highest-impact recovery tool.**

- Self-massage with foam roller, lacrosse ball, or massage gun — 10–20 min after intense sessions
- Professional massage 1–2× per month if budget allows
- Best targets: quads, hamstrings, glutes, lats, pecs

**Cold water immersion (ice baths)**:
- 10–15 min at 10–15°C (50–59°F)
- Best for inflammation control
- **Caution**: regular post-lifting cold can blunt muscle gains. Use sparingly during a hypertrophy phase
- Better timing: 4+ hours after lifting, or on rest days

**Compression garments**:
- Tights, sleeves, socks during/after exercise
- Modest benefit, no downside

**Stretching, electrostimulation**:
- Minimal evidence for DOMS relief
- Stretch for mobility goals, not recovery

## Part 6: Recovery During a Caloric Deficit

### Why Recovery Suffers in a Cut

- Lower glycogen replenishment
- Reduced muscle protein synthesis if protein is too low
- Hormonal shifts (lower testosterone, higher cortisol over time)
- Sleep disruption if deficit is too aggressive

### Mitigations

1. **Protein**: 2.0–2.4 g/kg/day (more than maintenance phase)
2. **Sleep**: 7–9 hrs/night
3. **Carb timing**: more carbs around training sessions
4. **Deload more often**: every 4–6 weeks instead of 8
5. **Cut volume slightly if needed** — 10% reduction beats skipping sessions
6. **Don't increase cardio too fast** — adds recovery demand

### Signs You're Under-Recovering

- Persistent soreness >72 hours after a session
- Strength drops 10%+ for 2+ weeks
- Sleep disturbed (waking, racing heart, shallow sleep)
- Mood: irritable, low motivation
- Resting heart rate elevated 5+ bpm above baseline
- Frequent minor illnesses

If 2+ of these appear, take a deload week or add 200–300 calories/day for 1–2 weeks.

## Part 7: Ankle History (Past Injury Considerations)

### Background

A 5-year-old ankle injury still influences current training risk. Old joint injuries leave behind:
- Subtle proprioceptive deficits (balance, awareness)
- Reduced range of motion if not addressed
- Compensatory patterns up the chain (knee, hip, lower back)

### Maintenance Drills

**Daily (2–3 min):**
- Single-leg balance, eyes open: 30 sec each leg
- Single-leg balance, eyes closed: 15 sec each leg (advanced)
- Ankle circles: 10 each direction, both feet

**2–3× per week:**
- Calf raises with full range: 3 × 10–15
- Tibialis raises (toes up against wall): 3 × 15
- Heel walks and toe walks: 30 sec each

### Running Form Cues

- Land mid-foot, not heel-first
- Avoid running on cambered (sloped) surfaces
- Replace shoes every 500–800 km
- Strength train ankles to bulletproof the joint

## Part 8: Sleep — The Master Recovery Tool

| Variable | Target |
|----------|--------|
| Duration | 7–9 hrs/night |
| Consistency | <30 min variation in bedtime |
| Heavy meals | Avoid 2 hrs before bed |
| Screens | Reduce 1 hr before bed |
| Caffeine | None after 2 PM |
| Room temp | Cool (16–19°C / 60–67°F) |
| Light | Dark (blackout if possible) |

### What Sleep Does

- Releases growth hormone (peak in deep sleep, early in night)
- Consolidates motor learning (skill acquisition — critical for calisthenics)
- Restores nervous system
- Reduces inflammation
- Regulates appetite hormones (helpful during a cut)

**Practical rule**: if you have to choose between 6 hours of sleep and a workout, sleep wins.

## Part 9: Warm-up Protocols (Injury Prevention)

### General Warm-up (5 min)

- 3 min light cardio (bike, row, jog)
- 2 min dynamic stretches (arm circles, leg swings, torso rotations)

### Specific Warm-up (Compound Lifts)

For each major compound lift:
- Empty bar × 10 reps
- 50% × 5 reps
- 70% × 3 reps
- 85% × 1 rep
- Working sets

### Shoulder-Specific Warm-up (Before Upper Body)

1. Wall slides × 10
2. Cable external rotation × 12 each side (light)
3. Banded face pulls × 15
4. Y/T/W holds × 1 round

5–8 minutes total.

## Part 10: When to See a Professional

### Self-Manage

- Mild soreness or stiffness
- Pain that fades within 48 hours
- Discomfort during one specific exercise (substitute it)
- Light pinching that disappears after warm-up

### See a Physiotherapist

- Pain >7 days despite reduced load
- Sharp pain during basic movements
- Range of motion lost >20%
- Numbness or tingling
- Visible swelling or bruising
- Pain that wakes you at night

### See an Orthopedist

- Acute injury with immediate weakness
- Suspected tear (sudden pop + immediate dysfunction)
- No improvement after 6+ weeks of physiotherapy
- Suspected fracture or joint instability
